# State of Vibe Coding Quality 2026: We Scanned 47 Apps. 57% Fail.

We scanned 47 of the most popular developer tools and startups. 57% fail basic quality standards.

Not "could use improvement." Not "a few things to fix." Fail. As in, your users are hitting broken experiences, your app is leaking security headers, and screen reader users literally cannot use your product.

Here's what we found.

## The Numbers

We pointed PreShip at 47 sites across three categories: established developer tools, Product Hunt launches, and AI-built applications. Each site got the full scan — accessibility, security, performance, SEO, privacy, and mobile.

The average score across all 47 sites: **66 out of 100**.

Total violations detected: **8,671**.

That's roughly 184 violations per site. Some had hundreds. A few had thousands.

27 out of 47 sites — **57%** — scored below our quality threshold. More than half.

## The Accessibility Crisis

This is the part that keeps us up at night.

The average accessibility score across all 47 sites: **35 out of 100**.

Thirty-five. That's not a rounding error. That's a systemic failure.

Missing alt text. Broken form labels. No keyboard navigation. Zero ARIA landmarks. Color contrast violations everywhere. We're talking about the basics — the stuff that's been documented for two decades.

And these aren't hobby projects. These are funded startups. Developer tools used by millions. Products built by teams that should know better.

When your accessibility score is 35, you're telling roughly 16% of the population — over a billion people worldwide — that your product isn't for them. You're also telling plaintiff attorneys that you're an easy target. ADA digital accessibility lawsuits crossed 4,000 filings in 2024 and they're still climbing.

## Security and Performance

Security averaged **53 out of 100**. Missing CSP headers, no HSTS, exposed server versions, missing X-Frame-Options. The kind of stuff that takes a few lines of config to fix but nobody bothers with until something goes wrong.

Performance was the bright spot at **68 out of 100**. Not great, but at least most sites load. Frameworks like Next.js and Vercel's infrastructure are doing a lot of heavy lifting here. The problem is that teams treat "it loads fast" as the entire quality bar.

## The Winners

A few sites nailed it.

**Claude (100)** and **Perplexity (100)** both scored perfect hundreds. Clean markup. Proper security headers. Accessible components. These are AI companies — they clearly take their own web presence seriously.

**Figma (97)** came in just behind. No surprise there. A design tool company that actually follows design best practices? Makes sense.

These scores prove that hitting 90+ isn't some impossible standard. It's achievable. It just requires giving a damn.

## The Losers

On the other end of the spectrum:

**Inbox Zero scored 0.** Zero. We double-checked. We scanned again. Still zero. We're not sure how you ship a publicly accessible product and score zero on a quality scan, but here we are.

**Retool (42)** and **Zapier (43)** are both well-funded, mature products with large teams. There's no resource excuse here. These are companies with hundreds of engineers shipping products that fail basic quality checks.

## Product Hunt vs. AI-Built

One of the more interesting findings: **Product Hunt launches averaged 58**, while **AI-built applications averaged 75**.

That gap surprised us. You'd expect vibe-coded apps to be messier. But the AI-built category — tools like Claude, Perplexity, v0 — actually ships cleaner code than the average Product Hunt launch.

Our theory: AI companies are hyper-aware of the "AI slop" criticism. They over-index on quality to prove the tech works. Meanwhile, Product Hunt launches prioritize speed to market over everything else. Ship fast, fix later. Except "later" never comes.

## What This Means for Vibe Coding

Vibe coding isn't going away. More software will be generated by AI, not written by hand. That's fine. The problem isn't AI-generated code. The problem is that nobody checks it.

LLMs are trained to produce code that works. Not code that works for everyone. Not code that's secure by default. Not code that follows WCAG guidelines. You get functional output, and functional output ships.

The gap between "it works on my screen" and "it works for everyone, safely" is where the 8,671 violations live.
